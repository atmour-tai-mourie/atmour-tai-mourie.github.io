Emotional Intelligence

What is Emotional Intelligence?

Emotional Intelligence is the intersection between the cognitive and emotional systems of a person. Being emotionally intelligent is being aware and proactive regarding the emotions of yourself and others, along with strong relationship managements skills. Developed in 1985 by Wayne Payne in his doctorial thesis "A Study of Emotion: Developing Emotional Intelligence", then popularised by Daniel Goleman 1985, the idea has been around for a little while now. Goleman describes five components of emotional intelligence, something I find helpful to keep in mind, knowing one's emotions, managing emotions, motivating oneself, recognize emotions of others, and handling relationships.

How is it different to IQ?

IQ is a measure of ability in intellectual pursuits such as comprehension and application of skillsets alongside ability to learn, measured by a set of standardised assessments. While IQ is largely associated with general academic ability, emotional intelligence revolves around awareness of ones self and others. Although a high IQ can be factor in success, and even though there are difficulties in attributing high performance to IQ or emotional intelligence (or even defining high performance or success in my opinion), there are some clear benefits to emotional intelligence that IQ cannot provide. So even if someone possesses a high IQ, emotional intelligence is still important to improving many factors of a persons work or personal life.

Why is emotional intelligence important?

Emotional Intelligence is important for the benefits it can provide relating to work performance, life satisfaction, personal relationships and other areas. By improving how we act, react and interact with those around us, we can improve in all the aforementioned areas.
